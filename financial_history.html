1971: The Nixon Shock

Breaking the Golden Anchor

On August 15, 1971, President Richard Nixon announced that the United States would no longer convert dollars to gold at a fixed value ($35/oz). This effectively ended the Bretton Woods system and turned the US Dollar into a fiat currency backed only by government decree.

The Result? Unleashed from gold, money printing began to accelerate, and the definition of "money" changed forever.

Exponential Money Growth

Without a gold constraint, the money supply (M2) began to skyrocket. From under $700 Billion in 1970 to over $22 Trillion today.

  • ⚠️ 2008 Crisis: Quantitative Easing begins.
  • ⚠️ 2020 Pandemic: 40% of all US Dollars in existence were printed in just two years.
1973: The Petrodollar Rise

Oil: The New Backing

To maintain demand for the Dollar after decoupling from gold, the US agreed with Saudi Arabia to price oil exclusively in USD. This created the "Petrodollar" system: every country needed dollars to buy energy, artificially propping up the currency's value.

Black Gold vs. Real Gold

While Oil prices (Blue line) are volatile in dollar terms, notice the Gold price (Yellow line).

The Insight: Priced in Gold, oil has actually remained relatively stable over 50 years. The volatility isn't necessarily in the oil; it's in the dollar itself.

The Silent Thief: Inflation

This chart shows the real purchasing power of $1 from 1970 to today. A dollar from 1970 is worth roughly 12 cents today.

This is why holding assets (Real Estate, Stocks, Gold, Bitcoin) has been crucial for wealth preservation compared to saving cash.
